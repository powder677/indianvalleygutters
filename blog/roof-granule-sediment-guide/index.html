The Anatomy of Granular Depletion

Asphalt shingles are coated with ceramic-bonded mineral granules. These aren't decorative; they are UV-Radiation Shields. When the asphalt binder degrades from Pennsylvania's extreme temperature cycles, these granules detach and settle into your gutters as a gritty sediment.

Level 1: Normal

Manufacturing runoff. Common on new roofs for 12 months.

Level 2: Warning

1/8" of grit. UV protection is failing. 2-5 years of life remain.

Level 3: Critical

1/4" of grit. Total granular loss. Roof failure imminent.

Abrasive Slurry: How Grit Kills Gutters

When roof sediment remains in your aluminum gutters, it creates a corrosive abrasive slurry. Roof granules are ceramic stone. As water flows over them during every PA rainstorm, they act like sandpaper, wearing away the protective coating inside your gutters.

60% Capacity Loss

Sediment doesn't sit loosely; it compacts. Just 1/2 inch of compacted grit in a standard 5-inch K-style trough reduces its hydraulic capacity by 60%. This forces water to back up under the shingles, accelerating the rot of your structural rafter tails.

The PA Humidity Factor

In Montgomery County towns like **Harleysville** and **Lansdale**, high humidity allows moss and lichen to anchor into the gaps left by missing granules.

  • Thermal Heave: Moss roots expand shingles, causing further granular shedding.
  • Hydrostatic Weight: Sodden sediment weighs 2-4 lbs per 10ft run, leading to "fastener creep" on historic Souderton fascia boards.
